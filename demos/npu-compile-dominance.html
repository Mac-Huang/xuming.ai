Does FeasibleAllocation actually dominate NPU compile time?

A controlled sweep on Intel NPU 5 (OpenVINO 2026.0) across Qwen2 (dense attn), ViT (vision encoder) · 28 cold-compile configurations · per-pass timing from the VPUX compiler's own report.

Short answer: no — not in general.

FeasibleAllocation (the memory allocator + list-scheduler) is the single heaviest pass in only 6 of 28 configurations. In 16 of 28 the heaviest pass is MultiClusterStrategyAssignment — the tiling / multi-cluster strategy search. Across the whole sweep FA ranges just 2.66%–33.36% of pass time. It only takes over at large model × large window: it needs ~7B params and a long prompt window before it dominates (reaching ~33% of the whole compile, ~44% at the stage level) — and then it plateaus: pushing the window to 8192 doesn't push it higher.

FA is #1 only ≥7B & W≥512 FA saturates ~33% — plateaus W1024→8192 tiling-search #1 for every small/mid compile ELF-serialize ≈ 20–28% floor everywhere FA flat ~5–8% for ≤3B even at W8192

The crossover: which stage owns the compile

Ordering every configuration by cold compile time (cheap → expensive) shows a clean hand-off. Tiling + strategy search owns the cheap compiles (~24–34%) and shrinks with scale; memory allocation + scheduling starts at ~11% and climbs to ~44% on the most expensive ones. ELF serialization is a steady floor throughout. FeasibleAllocation isn't a fixed bottleneck — it's the pass whose cost scales.

Why it scales — and where it stops: size × window, not modality

Within the dense Qwen2 family the FA share is flat ~5–8% up to 3B regardless of window, then on the 7B it climbs 5% → 10% → 33% as the window goes 512 → 1024. Two things must both be large — parameters and prompt window — because FeasibleAllocation schedules every tiled sub-operation through the NPU's tiny CMX, and the op count grows with both. ViT (a vision encoder, no KV cache) sits with the small models; it's size, not the attention-vs-vision distinction, that moves the needle.

But it saturates — it doesn't run away. Pushing the window all the way to W = 8192, the 7B's FA share peaks at ~33% (W1024) then plateaus (31% → 32% → 28% at W2048/4096/8192), and the cold compile time flattens with it (~230–260 s across W1024–8192). Once the working set far exceeds CMX the spill pattern is saturated, so more window scales the schedule linearly and FA's share holds constant instead of growing. And the smaller models still never cross over even at W8192 — FA stays 3–8% (on the 0.5–1.5B it even declines as the mechanical/serialize passes take a bigger slice). So the FA-dominant regime is a bounded plateau gated by model size, not an unbounded blow-up.

Which architectures even compile on NPU 5?

Before profiling, each family has to compile at all — and that turns out to be a real filter. We tried five architecture families; only dense-attention decoders and vision encoders compile. Both sparse-MoE and hybrid-SSM models fail, and for the same underlying reason: they emit data-dependent dynamic shapes (variable tokens-per-expert; SSM state) that the NPU's fully-static compiler can't lower.

So the dominance results above are, necessarily, the story for the architectures the NPU can run — and that class is narrower than the model zoo suggests.

What this says to optimize

The bottleneck is regime-specific, so the lever depends on your workload:

  • Small/mid models (≤3B, any window): the target is the tiling / multi-cluster strategy search — and it runs fully serial ( MultiClusterStrategyAssignment user/wall = 1.0×), so it won't get faster on more cores. Caching or pruning the strategy search is the lever.
  • Large models with long windows (≥7B, W≥1024): the target is FeasibleAllocation — a greedy simulate-and-spill list scheduler that re-runs 2–3× and is only mildly parallel. Bounding the window or shipping precompiled blobs keyed by (model, W) avoids it entirely.
  • Every compile: ELF serialization is an unavoidable ~20–28% floor — the cheapest win is not recompiling at all (warm blob cache).
