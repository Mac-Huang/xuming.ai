Project Statement: Beyond the Coded Gaze

Building this project felt like a wake-up call for our team. In our lectures for LIS 500, we often discuss the ethics of AI in the abstract, but sitting in front of a camera and trying to "teach" a machine how to recognize a human gesture forced us to confront these power dynamics head-on. We started with something simple, almost playful: hand gestures. Using Google’s Teachable Machine, we spent hours posing—holding up open palms, peace signs, thumbs up, and thumbs down.

As we went through the motions, the ideas from Joy Buolamwini’s Unmasking AI stopped being just "course reading" and started feeling like a lived reality. We noticed how the machine’s "understanding" of us changed based on the smallest variables. For Vesa, the machine was constantly distracted by her rings—some large, some small—which added a layer of complexity the machine wasn't prepared for. We realized that our environments—the specific furniture behind us, the shifting afternoon light in our rooms—weren't just backgrounds; they were part of the data the machine was using to define "us."

This was where the "Coded Gaze" became visible. We are only three people. No matter how many hundreds of photos we uploaded, our dataset remained biased by design. We couldn't possibly represent every race, gender, skin tone, or physical ability. We realized that while the machine might work perfectly for us in our specific rooms, it might fail someone else entirely simply because they don't look like us or live in a room lit like ours. This was a profound lesson in how unintentional bias is: we didn't mean to exclude anyone, yet our limited experiences became the machine's limited world.

The stakes felt higher when we pivoted our thinking from "silly hand gestures" to the real world. If a machine struggles to recognize a peace sign because of a ring or a shadow, what happens when that same technology is used for facial recognition at a border crossing, or to unlock a phone, or to determine "risk" in a courtroom? When these systems fail to accurately represent diverse populations, the consequences aren't just technical errors—they are human rights issues.

We also learned that accuracy is a moving target. Our model only started feeling "smart" after we each pushed past 100 photos, constantly going back to retrain it under different lighting. Even then, the machine remained inconsistent. There were moments of brilliance and moments of complete failure. This inconsistency served as a warning: we live in a world where many people—including our own friends and family—blindly trust AI results without a second thought. But this project taught us to look behind the curtain.

Ultimately, this experiment allowed us to see firsthand how easily a machine can misinterpret even the most basic human information. We hope that by sharing this project and citing works like Unmasking AI, we can encourage others to view AI as a tool to be questioned, rather than an authority to be relied upon. In a world where AI isn't going anywhere, our best defense is a critical eye and a diverse dataset.
